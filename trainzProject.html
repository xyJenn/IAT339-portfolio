tRAINz REVISED

Classic tRAINz revisioned

Contributers

Steven Kobrza · Keith Leung · Xiu Ying Chen · Julia Yee

2020 Oct

Game Overview

The objective of tRAINz is to travel along the tracks and obtain ores to trade gems with the merchant. Each gem is worth a different number of points. The goal is to trade with the merchant and gain 10 points. The first player who reaches 10 points wins.

People fun after analyzing other family friendly board games like the original Ticket to Ride, Monopoly, Clue, and others, we concluded that they are relatively neutral in design, with aims to appeal to a wide audience.

Mechanics
    Primary Mechanics and How they Support Gameplay Activities
  • Rolling dice: Players roll dice to move on the gameboard.
  • Mining ores: Player collect ores when land on the respective tiles Need ores to trade with merchant to win the game
  • Trade ores for gems with merchant: Each type of gem has a value attached to it. First to accumulate 10 points win.
Observation

We saw two major categories of changes that would greatly improve the state of our game. Firstly and most obviously, balance was an issue. Designing a board game meant that we had a lot of control over how the mechanics worked, without needing to worry about coding and graphics. With freedom to add mechanics comes headaches trying to balance them. We constantly fired ideas back and forth at each other to patch up problems we noticed, but new balancing issues arose.

The second major problem was managing randomness. Occasionally, each player had a shot to win the game. Other times, a player would accelerate way past the others, or some players lagged behind dramatically. The randomness in our game is a bit out of control and needs to be toned down in order to tame the player experience. For example, in one game, I had enough resources to perform 10 actions in a turn. My opponent, who wasn’t so lucky, drew over her hand and discarded half her hand. These actions were completely up to luck, and caused her to have a bad experience.

Conclusion

We learned that making a board game takes patience and a lot of testing. You’re going to come up with great ideas, and then you’re going to have to get rid of them. There might be some ideas you’re not willing to let go of, even when a team member has proposed a more functional alternative. We also learned how difficult it was to balance. A game that is too simple may lack gameplay dynamics, but a game that is sufficiently complex will require lots of testing and balancing. This is why, according to veteran game designers, board game developers pay top dollar to send their game to experienced game testers multiple times to get feedback on their game.

It was surprisingly easy to get a working prototype into Tabletop Simulator. However, we still felt like it was a barrier to testing our concepts. We imagine that using placeholder pieces on a board drawn on a piece of paper would be an easier way to test primitive game concepts before moving forward with them. Additionally, it’s easier to convey information on physical cards and surfaces. Camera angles and screen resolution posed as problems for legibility of written information. It would be easier to convey game rules on a physical surface and face-to-face communication. Finally, it wasn’t quite as fun to play board games online. The social aspect of playing board games in person can’t be replaced with virtual experiences (quite yet). We look forward to potentially playing some of these games in person when school opens up again.
